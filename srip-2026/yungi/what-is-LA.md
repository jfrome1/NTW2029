# What is Learning Analytics? by Yungi

Over the past decade, the field of Learning Analytics (LA) has been rapidly growing, from a purely theoretical concept to a concrete field of inquiry whilst also maintaining a global community of researchers and practitioners. Nevertheless, despite the huge growth of the field, the universal definition of learning analytics remains vague even for professionals, which makes a wide variety of interpretations across this discipline. Literal definitions, such as “the analytics of learning”, fail to provide clarity because they shift focus onto two competing terms. Defining “learning” is complicated because experts disagree on whether it is an individual mental process or a shared social activity. Meanwhile, the term “analytics” remains equally vague. Its historical roots are tied to the properties of prime numbers rather than applied data analysis, leaving the modern boundary between “analytics” and “analysis” heavily muddied. Rather than relying on a strict dictionary definition, learning analytics is best understood holistically by examining it across four conceptual dimensions : concern, opportunity, field of inquiry, and community. 

Viewed first as a concern, learning analytics was known to address the operational crisis brought by the massive amount of digital data within learning environments, where the speed, quantity, and variety of accumulated data surpassed the capacity of organizations to make sense of it. Through the joint efforts of academic research and industry, the purely technical challenges of large-scale data acquisition and tracking have become largely manageable. This was well demonstrated during the COVID-19 pandemic when people used Open Online Courses (MOOCs) and remote instruction platforms, where people proved that capturing and analyzing huge amounts of interaction data was “possible”. However, the primary complications have now shifted from technical infrastructure to adaptive challenges, as institutional adoption remains a substantial hurdle and successful cases for large-scale implementations are still difficult to find. The focus has shifted beyond data logistics to mature socio-ethical issues, such as student privacy, data ownership, and algorithmic fairness. This shows that educational data is tied to the technology used to gather it, which makes us question the relationship between humans and machines in the classroom. There is a clear conflict here: while learning is a creative and social experience, data analysis can be overly simple and risks losing the real human connection between teachers and students.

Conversely, the second dimension frames learning analytics as an opportunity to revolutionize educational systems by replacing human intuition with data-driven, actionable insights. This closely mirrors the historical evolution of Business Analytics (BA), adopting the premise that leaving educational data uncollected or unanalyzed represents a massive missed opportunity for intervention. Historically, this operationalized data shift began in the 1980/90s when university finance offices began utilizing computational methods, where this method also got transferred to the registrar offices to handle education-specific data. By the 2000s, these practices evolved into the specialized field known as “academic analytics”, utilizing sophisticated modeling to forecast student enrollment, retention, and dropout risks. The Learning Management System (LMS) was the catalyst for modern LA since it uniquely utilized the day to day educational utility with centralized student activity tracking. Crucially, while BA tracks metrics for corporate profits, LA is a more distinct research-driven field. Its insights are designed to feed directly “back” into the educational ecosystem, informing whether or not the teachers’ design for the education was good and providing students with immediate, localized metrics to optimize their own studying and collaboration. 

When analyzing learning analytics as a distinct field of inquiry, it faces an ongoing identity challenge because its core motivations are not unique. The motivations are shared across broad 21st century educational research. To hold the field together, it uses the "human in the loop" theory like a guiding principle. This theory assumes that since education is mainly a social activity, people must still make the final decisions. This idea stands against any plans to fully replace teachers with automated machines. Methodologically, LA is characterized by an exceptionally expansive approach, often referred to as “methodological agnosticism”. It lacks a single common language or strict Popperian doctrines of falsification, which technically causes it to fall short of traditional rigid sciences. Also, while this methodological openness may benefit the field by creating a big community and driving rapid membership growth, the lack of standard methods creates a trade-off that delays internal communication and makes it difficult for researchers to formulate universally convincing arguments to one another. Nevertheless, LA successfully differentiates itself from broad educational research because traditional research works on a long time scale with general impacts, while LA works on a short cycle, direct, and localized impact on the students being tracked. It is also historically separated from adjacent fields like Artificial Intelligence in Education (AIEd) or Educational Data Mining (EDM) by its cultural and developmental roots in 2000s LMS technologies rather than 1980s computerized systems or 1990s intelligent tutoring software. 

Finally, learning analytics must be understood as a community defined by its diverse participants. This diversity is reflected in formal educational programs, which range widely from highly technical and mathematical curricula to programs focused on practical problem solving, institutional administration, or educational psychology. This systemic diversity carries over into the current job market, where a real-world snapshot reveals that the dominant economic driver for LA employment is actually corporate training within corporate Human Resources departments, tracking and measuring the development of knowledge workers. In the workforce, Learning Analytics roles require proficiency in data management and visualization using tools like Tableau. These positions focus on generating reports and insights to support human decision-making, while full automation remains the responsibility of engineers rather than data analysts.

Ultimately, there is no singular, simple answer to what learning analytics really is, as it represents an intersection of evolving technologies, diverse professionals, and shifting ethical boundaries. Etymologically derived from the Greek phrase meaning “to set free” or “loosen”, the core promise of learning analytics lies in its capacity to loosen data trails to unlock entirely new domains of knowledge. While this foundational proposition remains attractive, the future maturation of the discipline depends on the community's ability to clearly define the exact parameters of educational improvement they seek, establish the most productive paths toward them, and generate compelling evidence of the positive changes made possible by the field.
