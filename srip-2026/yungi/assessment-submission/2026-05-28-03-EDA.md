# Explanatory Data Analysis of our Data from PostHog

Since I am more familiar of what kinds of data exist in our database, I used PostHog's API and Python to explore the data by trying to identify how it looks like, and possibly get some insights out of it. I also tried to answer some questions in the data-question-mapping.md file, to understand the data deeper and potentially help formulate the research direction / question.

## Executive Summary
Before deploying advanced stochastic or network-based models to analyze student trajectories, it is critical to ensure the underlying data accurately reflects reality. An audit of the raw PostHog event logs (**54,255 total events across 60 unique students**) reveals significant structural flaws in the tracking architecture, specifically regarding session fragmentation and passive tab-hoarding. 

This document outlines these limitations, quantifies their impact, and introduces the engineered reconstructions required to form a mathematically sound foundation for our core research questions.

---

## 1. The Coverage Gap: The Time-on-Page Limitation
**Addressed Concern:** *Question 1 & 4 (Time on Page and Walked-away visits)*

Calculating accurate "time-on-page" relies on capturing both the initial `$pageview` and the concluding `$pageleave` event. However, a significant portion of our data suffers from a coverage gap where the exit event is never recorded (e.g., when a student leaves a tab open until the session times out, or closes the browser entirely). 

* **Finding:** Approximately **30.1%** of all recorded `$pageview` events are "orphaned" and have no corresponding `$pageleave` event.
* **Conclusion:** Any metric relying on raw duration averages will systematically drop these sessions or heavily skew the data. Until visibility-tracking (`tabFocused` / `tabUnfocused`) is implemented in AY 2026-27, raw time-on-page should be treated as an approximate, heavily biased metric.

---

## 2. Isolating True Engagement: The Read-to-View Ratio
**Addressed Concern:** *Question 3 (False Engagement and Background Tabs)*

Because a `$pageview` fires whenever a page is loaded, raw view counts artificially inflate the popularity of pages used as passive background tabs. To isolate active learning, we mapped the `$pageview` events against `read` events (triggered when a student scrolls past 50% of the content) to calculate a **True Engagement Rate**.

### Engagement Discrepancy (Selected Top Traffic Pages)

| Page / Path | Total Pageviews | Total Reads | True Engagement Rate | Insight |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| `/course-ntw2029/schedule` | 3,176 | 1,576 | **49.6%** | High traffic, but heavily used as a quick-check or background tab. |
| `/` (Home) | 2,177 | 317 | **14.6%** | Primary springboard; rarely actively consumed. |
| `/assignments/papers/p2-p08-full` | 293 | 232 | **79.2%** | **High Intent:** When opened, this assignment is heavily read. |
| `/assignments/papers/p1-2510` | 367 | 226 | **61.6%** | **High Intent:** Active studying and consumption. |

* **Conclusion:** Navigational hubs (Home, Schedule) dominate raw traffic but show low actual engagement. Conversely, specific assignment pages command the highest focused attention. Moving forward, the `read_to_view_ratio` must be utilized to rank resource effectiveness.

## 3. Reconstructing Website Visits
**Addressed Concern:** *Question 2 (The `persistence: 'memory'` Flaw)*

The most critical tracking flaw in the current architecture is the `persistence: 'memory'` configuration. This setting forces PostHog to generate a new unique `$session_id` for every single page load, completely fracturing our ability to view a continuous study session. 

To resolve this, an algorithm was applied to reconstruct true "Website Visits" (continuous study trips) by grouping sequential events separated by **less than 30 minutes of inactivity**.

### The Fragmentation Fix
* **Total Flawed PostHog Sessions (Page-level):** 17,353
* **Total Reconstructed Website Visits (Trip-level):** 6,136
* **Fragmentation Ratio:** **2.8**

*PostHog was overcounting distinct sessions by a factor of nearly 3. By engineering the new `website_visit_id`, we have bypassed this limitation and restored the longitudinal integrity of the data.*

### Anatomy of a Reconstructed Study Session
With the sessions accurately reconstructed, we can observe the true nature of student behavior:
* **Median Events per Visit:** **5.0** * **Mean Events per Visit:** **8.8**
* **Maximum Events in a Visit:** **204.0**

* **Conclusion:** Typical student behavior is highly **transactional**. Half of all study sessions consist of 5 or fewer clicks—students log on with high intent, locate specific information, and leave. The right-skewed mean and extreme outliers (200+ clicks) highlight bursts of intense, non-linear navigation, likely corresponding to pre-deadline cramming.
