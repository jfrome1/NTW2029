# Using seqMAC Method to test it may be helpful

<h3>Methodologies</h3>

<h4>Everything was done using Python</h4>

- I made it so that the asynchronous PostHog event logs became a strict, time-ordered learning paths for every individual student (sequencing)
- stochastic mapping : instead of total clicks, I calculated the probability of a student transitioning between any two specific states (such as the likelihood of going from a Rage Click to Opening Nutshell).

- This transformed erratic web-surfing into a structured, computatble matrix.

- Then, I used machine learning and SHAP to not only predict student successs, but also forcing it to reveal exactly which specific navigationalk transitions drove its predictions.

- this experiment was done using dummy data (an uncleaned, unprocessed data from posthog) since I don't know if you would accept going forward with this methodology or not.
  
- If we go through the methodology explained at the top, we get this graph. On the left Y-Axis, we can see the specific transitions each students made and its frequency by looking at the color of dots (red : more frequent, blue : less frequent).
- The X-axis (SHAP value) tells us how the behavior affects the students' success. If the dots are more on the right, this is indicating that the student is more successful.

- Finally, the trainsitions are listed in order, where it tells us that the transition at the top mattered the most. As it goes down, the correlation between the transition and the students' success goes down as well.
