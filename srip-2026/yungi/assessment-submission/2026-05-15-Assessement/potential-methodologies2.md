# Methodologies that other researchers used to analyze students' interaction within educational websites and its insights

<h3>Using Google Analytics</h3>

- Researchers used Google Analytics to track user metrics like pageviews, bounce rates, and average time on page.
- They took this approach because Google Analytics automatically and dynamically captures session data across various devices, providing a ready-made framework to monitor how students interact with an online textbook without having to build a custom tracking system (contradicts to what we are doing)

- Generally, this method would be extremely helpful for website administrators to discover which content design choices work. For instance, this approach definitively revealed that giving instant feedback right after the quiz significantly increases the amount of time students spend engaging with a page, while non-graded has negative effects



<h3>Randomized Controlled Trials (RCT) with Learning Analytics Dashboards</h3>

- They use this rigorous approach that utilizes RCT combined with a two-stage least squares (2SLS) instrumental variable analysis to measure the causal effect of giving students access to a learning analytics dashboard (LAD)

- The dashboard uses machine learning algorithms to predict student's grades and progress depending on their performance as of now, compared to their peers (Would this help the NTW students?)

- They used this complex approach because simply offering a dashboard often leads to "self-selection bias" where highly motivated students use the dashboard more and get better grades.

- The 2SLS analysis proves that visual dashboards and early warning systems act as powerful meta-cognitive tools. Providing students with absolute and relative performance metrics creates a signaling function that increases their self-reflection and significantly boosts their performance on the online assessments.
