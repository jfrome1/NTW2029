These are reflections from students who completed Paper 2 last term that offer perspectives on that paper.
Don't read these as instructor guidance you must follow. These are suggestions from other students, and their experiences may differ from yours, but you may find some of their observations useful as you work through the assignment.

## Reading and understanding the articles

"My biggest advice would be to do multiple close readings of the target article and the commentary (especially the target article) before thinking about the analysis. Between P04 and P09, I did around 5 close readings of the target article, and I found something noteworthy that I hadn't previously noticed each time. The re-readings helped my analysis a lot."

"I think that being able to explain something clearer comes naturally with actually understanding the paper logically. I found it difficult to explain at the start, but after struggling and having spent over a long time on the materials, I find it is easier to explain things clearly. So do not be too harped up over writing succinctly at the start."

"Not think about writing the paper first, but rather focus on understanding the substance of the arguments through getting comfortable with confusion, deep thinking and analysis. Only once one has a semblance of direction should one start writing, and use the writing to purify and clarify the starting direction, which should already be good. If one starts off in a nonsensical direction, no amount of good writing can save you."

"I would tell future students to focus on the papers' individual arguments first rather than diving straight into the criticisms or disagreements, as that would paint a clearer picture on what beliefs are truly not compatible across both papers."

"After working through the paper, I understand the importance of making sure I understand clearly what both articles are claiming and where they stand to figure out the disagreement between the articles. One strategy that I found useful was to think from the point of view of the different parties. If I were the commentary article, why would I criticise the target or what changes do I wish to make? And if I were the target, would I agree to the criticisms and the changes?"

## Choosing your focus

"I would say to avoid choosing a disagreement because it looks 'easy'. Instead I would suggest choosing a disagreement that the commentary seems to take seriously, rather than using a criticism that serves their own purpose."

"Target the paper on a very small section of each article to ensure that the scope is sufficiently narrow."

"I would suggest future students to nail down on an explicit disagreement early on so that you can then spend more time thinking about what stems from the disagreement for a better analysis."

"I would suggest that other students avoid choosing a topic they do not feel interested in because they will spend a lot of time writing about it."

## Identifying the disagreement

"When identifying disagreements between target articles and commentaries, it helps to explicitly phrase the disagreement in the form of what each side claims or believes first, before going into why they diverge, especially when forming theses. This approach helps me separate the surface-level dispute from its underlying cause and keeps me from jumping straight to causal analysis."

"I would advise future students to have an open mind when analyzing the target and commentary articles. I had much challenge in identifying the underlying reason driving the disagreement because I viewed ordinary terms such as 'appeal' in the way I would usually understand it. However, it was only until I learned to be more sensitive when tracing back to what could have possibly been the reason for both scholars to take such different explanatory aims that I found out came from a mismatch in their understandings."

"I would also tell future students to avoid sticking onto one set disagreement but rather explore all possible disagreements, which would then allow them to truly see what is the deeper disagreement."

"I think write out a main outline for each paper first, be able to understand the main structure of each of their arguments. So then we can see nuanced differences within the bigger picture, it might give them a more intuitive understanding of their disagreements."

## Managing the process

"I would advise future students to intentionally set aside time in periodic chunks to approach this paper. This is because the thinking process takes time, and writing the paper in an iterative manner is much more helpful in making links or stumbling upon new revelations as opposed to trying to cram the analysis within a rush period before the deadline. Additionally, the learning process is much more enjoyable when you take it slow and focus more on the understanding and learning rather than completing the task."

"I would remind future students to stick to the basic principles of writing that we learned in paper 1 and to remain unafraid when they feel like they have nothing to add to the conversation, because the thesis genuinely emerges from long term analysis."

"Don't be afraid to rewrite everything upon developing some better intuition after an initial try. Abandoning existing work can often allow a much better version to be reborn from intuition."

"I think I would encourage them to pen down arguments in a flow chart or write them down in a more visual way. A lot of time was used in this paper trying to gather many disparate points by both authors."

"The most important piece of advice I can give (that I did not do, but wish I did) is to consult Prof even in between peer reviews and conferences."

"I would recommend asking your peer reviewers to try explaining what they read back to you, so you can see where you need to make your explanations clearer."

## Writing clearly

"Students have a bad tendency to utilise complex vocabulary and concepts in order to impress the reader or the professor. This practice results in them writing very vaguely and simply not conveying the main idea of the argument in an accurate way. I would recommend students to focus on developing the idea and the argument rather than simply enhancing the vocabulary of their paper."

"Students should try to question 'why' when making statements, so as to avoid making unsupported statements. Students should avoid having multiple ideas in one sentence as it does not add to the readability of their paper."

"A good strategy to use is to make sure you do not have any unclear referent terms and use simple language whenever possible. I also found it useful to explain to myself (or other people) what I thought the main disagreement was."

"I would advise students to use more quotations to explain their paragraphs. Since this paper requires more analysis than paper 1 it requires us to look at external sources, quoting them, and drawing implicit connections between the sources and the main disagreement. This skill is a lot harder than expected, and not really needed as much in paper 1."

## Using AI tools

Students had different experiences with AI tools. Notice how they reached different conclusions based on their own use:

"Do not rely on ChatGPT for now, at least. ChatGPT is bad at academic writing because it changes a lot of words and definitions that should have remained consistent in academic writing without noticing you. Also, I realized that most of the time ChatGPT unconditionally agreed with you, even though there is an obvious flaw in your argument. That is one of the reasons why I stopped using ChatGPT to even refine my argument in Paper 2. That being said, ChatGPT is useful for looking for ideas, but that's all."

"Do not use ChatGPT for getting an overall summary of the research papers. Use ChatGPT only for specific clarifications you want to make. And make sure what ChatGPT says fits in with your overall understanding of the paper. You will save more time wrestling with the research, line by line, than being lazy and using ChatGPT. Because in the end you will have to fix the issues ChatGPT makes, and it can be difficult to spot the nuanced mistakes. The mistaken explanations ChatGPT gives you would make you more confused."

"Initially, I used to use AI more for my NTW assignments because I thought that the best way to use AI was for it to give me its own original content and then I can tailor it to my expectations. However, I realised that AI is a better teacher than a student. So, towards the second half of my NTW, I used significantly less AI but instead fully came up with essays and research on my own then made GenAI act as a teacher and provide feedback for my work."

## What students discovered about scholarly disagreements

"Scholarly disagreements are often extremely nuanced, and the source of the disagreement may be completely different from the explicit disagreement itself. In the case of my article, the disagreement seemed to be in the explicit arguments proposed by both authors, but the true source of their disagreement lay in their interpretations of [a concept]. I would not have been able to identify such nuance before this course."

"I was not a big reader of scholarly disagreements until the start of this course. As a result, I naturally assumed that when a scholar disagrees with another scholar, they are always disagreeing on the main argument itself. However, writing this paper made me realise a lot of the scholarly disagreements stem from an underlying disagreement of a certain key word or term, which can often even be implicit."

"I learned that many scholarly disagreements are not about facts but about what exactly is being measured or tested. Some scholars may strengthen others' claims so that it is easier to generate a clean contradiction, but this kind of contradiction is actually based on misalignments between construct definitions and operationalizations."

"Before writing this paper, I often simply trusted that articles by scholars could be taken as true, and held the opinion that the concepts they discuss are far too complex for me to try and unpack. Writing this paper helped me realise how human these scholars actually are, and how sometimes disagreements can stem from simple everyday things like biased perspectives or misreadings. In addition, I have realised that scholarly papers are much less daunting than they initially appear to be, especially if I put in the time and focus on understanding the concepts."

"I have come to recognise that explicit arguments depend on many assumptions, some of which may not agree. Positions may appear to disagree on a conceptual or empirical standpoint, but they may frame a mechanism differently or presuppose different causes which affect their a priori theoretical backing."
