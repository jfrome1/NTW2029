## DUBOURG COMMENTARIES

### 1. Dunk & Mar - Cognitive exploration drives engagement

**Key criticisms:**
1. **Evidence problems:** Empirical evidence for exploration is "mostly absent or contradictory"
2. **Non-empirical support:** Evidence cited is often non-empirical or unpublished (conference presentations, theoretical conjecture)
3. **Empirical contradiction:** Extraversion negatively correlates with imaginary worlds (contradicts theory's prediction that spatial exploration/Extraversion should predict appeal)
4. **Prediction reversal:** Theory predicts Plasticity (Openness + Extraversion) should predict appeal, but only Openness does
5. **Re-engagement problem:** Re-consumption of known worlds contradicts exploration drive (exploration requires novelty, not re-visiting)
6. **Alternative explanation:** Openness (cognitive exploration) better explains appeal than overall exploratory preference

**Main thrust:** Evidence contradicts predictions; Openness (cognitive) not Plasticity/Extraversion (spatial) explains appeal

---

### 2. Norman & Goldstein - All non-real worlds provide exploration

**Key criticisms:**
1. **Scope problem:** Fails to answer "Why Imaginary Worlds?" - doesn't explain why fantastical vs. historical fiction
2. **Framework too narrow:** Theory excludes historical fiction without justification (can't visit Renaissance Europe any more than Middle Earth)
3. **Eurocentrism:** Theory doesn't account for cultural variance in children's pretend play (Mayan, Taiwanese, hunter-gatherer, Mennonite children)
4. **Misidentifies mechanism:** Being "imaginary" (any unreal) provides psychological distance and quarantining, not specifically "fantastical"
5. **Developmental evidence:** Young children prefer realistic over fantastical fiction (contradicts prediction that high fantasy maximizes exploration)
6. **Distance vs. fictionality:** Psychological distancing works for ANY fiction (including realistic), not just highly fantastical

**Main thrust:** "Imaginary" (non-real) is what matters, not "fantastical" (far from reality); theory too narrow and Eurocentric

---

### 3. Nyhout & Lee - Young children are not driven to explore imaginary worlds

**Key criticisms:**
1. **Developmental prediction fails:** Children don't prefer imaginary worlds despite having highest exploratory drive
2. **Evidence limited:** Market data misleading (parents control); cited study only includes adults (18.5-32.7 years)
3. **Reality bias:** Preschoolers prefer realistic to fantastical fiction in consumption AND production
4. **Processing difficulty:** Children struggle to represent spatial information in imaginary worlds (contradicts wayfinding account)
5. **Real-time comprehension:** Children can't integrate fantastical information during real-time processing
6. **Prediction inversion:** If exploration function is to learn about real world, imaginary worlds would be counterproductive for children

**Main thrust:** Developmental evidence contradicts prediction that imaginary worlds most appealing when exploratory drive highest (early childhood)

---

### 4. Shtulman - The familiar appeal of imaginary worlds

**Key criticisms:**
1. **Conceptual tension:** "Novel" in foraging means new instances of familiar resources, not entirely new resources
2. **Counterintuitive limits:** Too many counterintuitive ideas make stories incomprehensible (Grimm's "Girl Without Hands" fails)
3. **Intuitive backdrop:** Counterintuitive ideas only appealing against backdrop of intuitive ones
4. **Constrained magic:** Even magical events follow intuitive logic (heavy objects harder to levitate; foundational laws harder to violate)
5. **Familiar core:** People prefer novel surface with familiar core (Star Wars planets, Pokemon genealogies) not truly novel ideas
6. **Rejects true novelty:** If seeking true novelty, would study quantum mechanics/differential equations instead of imaginary worlds

**Main thrust:** People seek familiar ideas with novel packaging, not true novelty; imaginary worlds confirm real-world intuitions

---

### 5. Moore & Hills - The evolution of imagination and the adaptive value of imaginary worlds

**Key criticisms:**
1. **Too narrow conception:** Characterizing imaginary worlds as "hedonic but non-adaptive exaptation" misses adaptive value
2. **Ancient and adaptive:** Imagination is evolutionarily old (pre-mammalian) allowing simulation of action outcomes
3. **Not just entertainment:** Imaginary worlds serve adaptive functions: deliberation, goal construction, evaluation
4. **False distinction:** Separating fictional from religious narratives unhelpful (both are imaginary worlds)
5. **Comparative evidence:** Self-projection seen in non-humans (rats imagine routes via hippocampal place cells)
6. **Functional continuity:** Modern imaginary worlds functionally similar to ancestral imaginings (moral parables, existential conflicts)

**Main thrust:** Imagination and imaginary worlds are adaptive (not mere exploitation), ancient, and serve practical functions beyond entertainment

## SAVAGE COMMENTARIES

### 1. Harrison & Seale - Against unitary theories of music evolution

**Key criticisms:**
1. **Dismisses byproducts too quickly:** Music's universality doesn't prove independent function (could be byproduct of language's universality)
2. **Neural specialization explained:** Much neural specialization could be explained by neural plasticity
3. **Weak unitary claims:** Theory claims to be "unified" and "comprehensive" but doesn't disprove competing explanations (sexual selection, credible signaling)

# Nutshell vs Anchor Link Decision Criteria

## What Are These Elements?

### Nutshell Links

- Text-expansion links that appear inline with content
- Marked with `[:displayed text](#x-matching-header-id)` syntax
- Content appears right where student is reading when clicked
- Cannot be directly linked to in emails
- Best for optional depth and context

### Anchor Links (FAQs)

- Links to FAQ sections at the bottom of pages
- Can be emailed directly to students (e.g., `website.com/page#faq-late-submission`)
- Searchable with Ctrl+F
- Stand alone as complete answers to common questions
- Best for reusable answers to recurring questions

## CRITICAL: Sentence Rewriting When Converting Old Nutshell Links

### The Problem

Old-style nutshell links used fragments that grammatically completed the sentence. New-style links use complete descriptive phrases that cannot grammatically fit within a sentence.

### MANDATORY REWRITING PROCESS

YOU CANNOT SIMPLY CHANGE THE LINK TEXT. THE ENTIRE SENTENCE MUST BE REWRITTEN.

### Conversion Checklist

When converting old-style fragment nutshells to new descriptive style:

1. STOP - Do not attempt to just replace the link text
2. EXTRACT - Remove the nutshell link entirely from the sentence
3. COMPLETE - Write the sentence with the intended meaning in plain text
4. ADD - Create a new nutshell link that describes what additional information is available
5. VERIFY - Read the sentence aloud to ensure it's grammatically correct

### Why This Matters

Fragment-style links (old): The link text IS the content
Descriptive-style links (new): The link text DESCRIBES the content

This fundamental difference means sentence-level rewriting is ALWAYS required when converting between styles.
